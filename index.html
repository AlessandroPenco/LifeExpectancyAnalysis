Life Expectancy Analysis

Introduction

Life expectancy is a critical metric used to determine the average length of time a person is expected to live. The value of this metric varies widely across the world, with different countries and regions experiencing different trends in life expectancy. In developed nations, life expectancy is generally higher due to factors such as access to healthcare, better nutrition, and overall higher standards of living. However, in developing nations, life expectancy can be much lower, with many factors such as disease, poor nutrition, and lack of access to healthcare playing a role.

Map chart

We chose to use a map chart as our first visualization because it provides a comprehensive overview of life expectancy trends worldwide. This type of chart enables us to identify clear patterns in the distribution of life expectancy across the globe. As expected, we observed that life expectancy tends to be higher in more developed countries, while developing nations generally have lower life expectancies.

The map chart also allows us to track changes in life expectancy over time, highlighting the persistence of these trends across different years. With this visualization, we can gain a deeper understanding of how global socioeconomic factors influence life expectancy, and identify areas where targeted interventions may be necessary to improve health outcomes.

Line chart

To further investigate life expectancy trends, we chose to use a line chart as our next visualization. By plotting average life expectancy over time, we can gain insight into whether life expectancy is increasing, decreasing, or remaining stable.

To ensure that our line chart is easy to read and interpret, we decided to group the states into continents. This helps to avoid overcrowding the graph and allows us to clearly visualize trends in life expectancy for each continent. By doing so, we can identify patterns and disparities between different regions.

Bar chart

Our investigation into life expectancy continued with the use of a bar chart to compare life expectancies between genders. By examining this chart, we were able to gain insight into whether there are any significant differences in life expectancy between males and females.

Our findings revealed that, across all continents, females generally have a higher life expectancy than males. However, we also observed that this gap between male and female life expectancy is wider in developed countries compared to developing ones. This discrepancy can be attributed to the fact that less developed countries often face more adverse social and economic conditions, which can have a greater impact on female health outcomes.

Box chart

To further explore life expectancy trends across different continents, we utilized a more advanced visualization technique - a box plot. This type of plot not only displays the average life expectancy for each continent, but also provides information on the distribution of life expectancy by showing the first and third quartiles, as well as the minimum and maximum values.

Our analysis revealed that developed continents generally have higher mean life expectancies and lower variability in life expectancy between the first and third quartiles, with minimum and maximum life expectancies also being higher than in developing countries. This suggests that there are underlying factors, such as better healthcare and higher standards of living, that contribute to higher life expectancy in developed continents.

We offer two views for this graph: the first identifies the state with the minimum life expectancy and state with the maximum life expectancy. The second visualization calculates the minimum and maximum values as min = q1 - 1.5 * interQuantileRange and max = q3 + 1.5 * interQuantileRange, which allows us to identify and visualize outliers.

Parallel line chart

To further explore the differences in life expectancy across different states and continents, we utilized a parallel line plot that represents each state individually and groups them by continent using color. To enhance the visualization and user experience, we implemented an interactive tooltip that highlights the selected continent, allowing for a more dynamic exploration of the data.

The parallel line plot revealed that there is significant variance in life expectancy between states belonging to the same continent, underscoring the importance of considering individual states when analyzing global health trends. Additionally, the plot highlighted disparities in development and health outcomes within continents themselves, further emphasizing the need for targeted interventions and policies aimed at improving health outcomes in specific regions.

Interestingly, our analysis revealed that the Asian continent has made significant progress in reducing the variance in life expectancy by developing previously underdeveloped states.

Stacked area chart

In our effort to provide a more inclusive and targeted analysis of life expectancy trends, we utilized a stacked area chart to focus on a specific population group - people with disabilities or terminal illnesses. By separating the average life expectancy into two areas - the green representing years lived in good health, and the orange representing years lived with illness or disability - we were able to gain a more nuanced understanding of the health outcomes for this vulnerable population.

Our analysis of the stacked area chart revealed that years lived with disabilities in developed states (10-11 years) were greater than those lived in developing states (7-9 years). This finding underscores the potential impact of socioeconomic factors on health outcomes for people with disabilities or terminal illnesses.

Bubble chart

Using a bubble chart, we've plotted life expectancy and GDP on the Cartesian axes, while the size of the bubbles represents an approximative value of the population. and the color identifies the continent to which each state belongs. These variables are all relative to a specific year.

Upon analyzing the chart, we observed that states with higher GDP tend to have a higher life expectancy, but this is not always the case. Other factors, such as access to healthcare, education, and socio-economic conditions, may play a role in determining life expectancy. Therefore, while GDP can be an indicator of development, it is not the only factor to consider when assessing a state's overall well-being.

Radar (Spider) chart

The Radar chart is a comprehensive visualization that combines several data points seen in previous visualizations. It allows us to relate total life expectancy, male and female life expectancy, GDP, and healthcare spending.

This enables us to visualize whether a higher standard of living and consequent higher healthcare spending allows the population to have a longer life expectancy. Through the chart, we can observe that there is a correlation between a higher life expectancy and a higher per capita healthcare spending.

However, as expected, the extension of life expectancy is not proportional to healthcare spending. For example, Europe, which invests significantly higher amounts in healthcare than other continents, has only slightly higher life expectancy.
